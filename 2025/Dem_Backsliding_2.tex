\section{Electoral Losers and Experts: A Marriage of Convenience?}


Democracy rarely dies to the sound of tanks \parencite{Levitsky:2018aa}; they might also fade behind legalese, independent commissions, and ``crisis cabinets'' staffed by experts \parencite{Levitsky2002}. In Finland, the COVID-19 pandemic made this trade-off vivid: trust in public health authorities and scientific advisers was high on average, yet a subset of voters---disproportionately out-partisans---grew skeptical when expert guidance arrived alongside the government's agenda. Other notable examples show that in polarized electorates, citizens often tolerate anti-liberal moves when their side benefits \parencite{Svolik2020, Svolik2019}. Classic work links electoral competition and cleavage structure to regime vulnerability \parencite{Sartori1976, Dahl1971, Lipset1981}, while contemporary research documents how incumbents tilt the field without canceling elections \parencite{Levitsky2010, Bermeo2016, Waldner2018}. Our paper pushes this literature one step earlier in the causal chain and asks: \emph{when do voters prefer the steady hand of experts over the rough justice of elections?} Our core claim is that technocratic delegation is ideologically valenced: when expert bodies are seen as aligned with incumbents, electoral losers are less willing to shift authority away from elected politicians. In doing so, we foreground ideological heterogeneity regarding expert delegation within losers' consent---a dimension that has received relatively little systematic attention.


We address this question by shifting attention from voters' reactions to democratic violations toward a prior and largely neglected choice: whether citizens are willing to delegate rule-making authority to unelected experts in the first place. We develop a simple voter-choice formal model in which individuals weigh the expected performance advantage of technocratic rule against the ideological loss of ceding authority away from electoral control. The model implies that support for technocratic delegation is ideologically conditional rather than uniform. When expert bodies are perceived as aligned with the incumbent government, electoral losers become less willing to delegate authority as their ideological distance from the governing coalition increases; when experts are perceived as neutral, this distance penalty attenuates. We test these implications using a novel 2025 survey of adults in Finland---a high-capacity, multiparty parliamentary democracy and thus a hard case where expert governance might otherwise be broadly acceptable \parencite{OECD2021}. We measure ideological proximity to government with an individual-level, seat-weighted index based on party closeness, and perceived government-expert alignment is proxied through a relative trust gap between the Cabinet and the Finnish Central Bank. Consistent with our argument and theory, we find that ideological distance depresses support for technocratic delegation primarily among respondents who infer that experts will follow the government's lead. In doing so, the paper complements work on partisan tolerance of democratic transgressions (\emph{a la} \cite{Svolik2020, Svolik2019}) by highlighting delegation to experts as an additional, demand-side margin through which democratic accountability can be weakened.

Our contribution refines existing perspectives on democratic norms and losers' consent. While oppositions often support institutional checks as political insurance against executive overreach \parencite{Capoccia2007a}, we show that support for technocratic delegation depends on whether expert institutions are perceived as aligned with the incumbent governing coalition. This scope condition shifts the focus from aggregate trust in experts to partisan perceptions of government-expert alignment, helping explain why technocratic governance can polarize electoral losers even in high-trust democracies. Consistent with prior research, we find that support for democratic norms is conditional \parencite{Saarinen2020}: some voters tolerate transgressions by ideologically congruent leaders \parencite{Saikkonen2022}, and preferences for expert decision-making concentrate among respondents who view ordinary citizens as less competent, whereas confidence in fellow citizens tilts preferences toward representative or participatory channels \parencite{Rapeli2023}.

Our account complements two adjacent strands of research. First, when citizens perceive an ideologically congruent and competent executive, they may endorse reallocating authority in ways that weaken constraints because expected policy gains loom large---what \textcite{Singer2018} describes as the ``pernicious effects'' of good representation and performance on tolerance for constraining rival actors. Second, recent work emphasizes that democratic stability depends not only on losers' consent but also on winners' restraint, which is activated only as violations accumulate rather than immediately \parencite{Werner2025}. Our focus remains on the losers' side of this equilibrium: when expert bodies are perceived as aligned with incumbents, ideological distance from government translates into lower willingness to delegate.

Finland constitutes a demanding test of this argument. It is a high-capacity, consensus-oriented polity where trust in public institutions and expertise is generally high. Precisely for this reason, it allows us to isolate heterogeneity in how citizens evaluate technocratic delegation. Our claim is not that Finns view experts as partisan in the aggregate, but that a subset of voters---especially those far from the governing coalition---infer alignment between expert institutions and the incumbent government when expert bodies advise, are appointed by, or implement the agenda of incumbents. In this framework, technocratic delegation is evaluated as a trade-off between perceived governance quality and ideological loss. Even in a high-trust environment, support for technocratic delegation declines with ideological distance from government when experts are perceived as government-aligned and attenuates when experts are seen as neutral. Overall, the observed pattern is driven by a subset of voters rather than by generalized anti-expert sentiment, situating the paper at the intersection of realist accounts of mass politics \parencite{Achen2016} and critiques of the administrative state \parencite{Hamburger2014, Glennon2014, Majone1994}.


\section{When Expertise Is Perceived as Partisan}

Democratic governance typically balances technocratic delegation against democratic accountability. Technocratic delegation refers to entrusting policy authority to experts or non-partisan institutions insulated from direct electoral control. Examples include independent central banks, regulatory agencies, or constitutional courts staffed by specialists. Such technocratic delegation is justified by the promise of neutral competence and long-term policy stability, but it inherently trades off responsiveness to the electorate. In other words, empowering the experts may improve technocratic quality at the cost of reducing the public's direct say in decision-making \parencite{Caramani2017}. This tension is well documented: technocratic policy-making can dilute traditional accountability, since decisions occur outside the arena of partisan competition and popular oversight \parencite{Bertsou2020}. Democratic accountability, by contrast, requires that those who make policy are answerable to voters through elections and representative institutions. The crux of the issue is that what incumbents defend as neutral expertise, electoral losers may perceive as unaccountable elite rule. 

Institutional independence need not equal neutrality. Preferences for technocratic delegation also vary along non-ideological lines: attitudes toward technocracy track beliefs about mass competence, helping explain why a subset of voters accepts expert rule even in high-trust contexts \parencite{Rapeli2023}. Performance and felt representation can legitimate authority shifts that sideline rivals \parencite{Singer2018}, whereas winners' vigilance tends to activate only as procedural violations accumulate \parencite{Werner2025}. Consequently, losers read technocratic delegation through a partisan lens, while winners' support is conditional and violation-sensitive. Even insulated bodies can be ``independent but not indifferent.'' For example, in the U.S., the Federal Reserve has behaved as a ``conditional inflation hawk,'' at times advantaging one partisan bloc \parencite{Clarks2013}. The implication for our theory is direct: losers' consent to technocratic delegation hinges on whether ostensibly neutral institutions are perceived as impartial or aligned with the incumbent governing coalition; although oppositions often back checks as insurance against executive power \parencite{Capoccia2007a}, a subset of voters resists technocratic delegation when expert bodies are seen as government-alignedŌĆöeven as they continue to endorse judicial or oversight constraints.

Losers' consent is central to democratic stability: those who lose must still accept the system and expect protection in opposition \parencite{Anderson2005a}. Acceptance is easier when independent institutions credibly guard fair play. When these expert bodies are perceived as partisan-stacked with or sympathetic to incumbents-consent to technocratic delegation collapses. Technocratic delegation then looks like a democratic end-run: unelected actors advancing the winnersŌĆÖ program. Accordingly, losers resist ceding authority to such bodies, invoking accountability and the ``will of the people.''


Our argument draws on several strands of research. In polarized polities, even nominally non-partisan institutions are viewed through partisan lenses. Polarization---the deep ideological divide between camps---erodes a shared baseline of institutional trust. \textcite{Svolik2019} shows that intense polarization ``undermines the public's ability to serve as a democratic check'': voters trade democratic principles for partisan gains. As a result, each side suspects that ostensibly neutral referees (judiciary, civil service, expert regulators) tilt toward the other camp. Empirically, support for liberal-democratic checks is increasingly sorted by partisanship: in countries governed by right-populist parties (Hungary, Poland, Brazil), government supporters express lower support for constraints, whereas opponents value them more \parencite{Claassen2025}. The upshot is mirror-image perceptionsŌĆö\emph{winners discount constraints, losers champion them, unless they believe the institutions are captured}. Under hyper-polarization, bodies meant to serve as neutral checks become, or are seen as, instruments of incumbent advantage; polarization thereby ``magnifies the partisan capture of independent institutions,'' corroding their legitimacy \parencite[p.~471]{Lieberman2019}.

%  1
Given this dynamic, electoral losers are most likely to oppose technocratic delegation when expert bodies carry ideological valence aligned with incumbents. By ideological valence we mean that expert institutions are \emph{perceived} not as neutral but as tilting toward one side of the spectrum. Expertise is never exercised in a vacuum: economic technocrats may hew to marketŌĆÉliberal orthodoxy \parencite{Clarks2013,Clark2013}; legalŌĆōbureaucratic elites may share the regimeŌĆÖs social outlook; scientific experts may prioritize values of one cultural camp. In polarized contexts, these associations become salient. Losers, then can come to view the ``independent'' central bank as effectively partisan (e.g., austerity that aligns with the government) or the judiciary as stacked with loyalists. What looks like a technocratic decision---an impartial publicŌĆÉhealth directive or a monetary tightening---is then read through a partisan lens. Perceived bias shortŌĆÉcircuits trust: technocratic delegation appears less like neutral expertise and more like entrenchment behind a facade of objectivity.

%  2
Partisans also apply a double standard to ostensibly ``neutral'' oversight: support for checks is strongest when they restrain the outŌĆÉparty and softens when they bind coŌĆÉpartisans \parencite{Svolik2019, Kingzette2021}. This aligns with the realist view that group identity and performance, not abstract proceduralism, structure mass attitudes \parencite{Achen2016}. Thus, both winners and losers champion checks when useful and discount them when costly. An outŌĆÉpartyŌĆÖs demand for accountability can therefore mask outcome concerns: ``unelected bureaucrats'' or ``activist judges'' draw fire when they are perceived as aligned with the rival camp. Our argument links this demandŌĆÉside asymmetry to backsliding: just as incumbents may erode checks to entrench power, oppositions may withdraw support from institutions they deem compromised, making commitment to rules contingent on who controls state authority \parencite{Waldner2018}.

%  3
Opposition to technocratic delegation should be most acute under two conditions. First, when polarization and partisan identity are strong: mutual mistrust is high, and even independent institutions are painted red or blue; compromise outside electoral contest is treated as loss. Under such polarization, the baseline for losersŌĆÖ consentŌĆöconfidence that the system treats sides fairlyŌĆöerodes. Second, when incumbents have politicized or captured nominally technocratic bodies. Governments often use appointments or institutional reforms to bias independent agencies during backsliding episodes \parencite{Luhrmann2019}ŌĆöpacking courts, tilting election commissions, staffing centralŌĆÉbank boards with ideologues. These moves become focal points for conflict; oppositions then resist further empowerment of those bodies, sometimes refusing to recognize captured referees. Where integrity is compromised, losers predictably reject technocratic delegation.

%  4
Crucially, our theory does not claim that electoral losers oppose expert rule \emph{per se}. Support for technocratic delegation is conditional on perceived neutrality relative to partisan competition. Where expert institutions enjoy crossŌĆÉpartisan trust, losers may willingly delegateŌĆöindeed, oppositions have historically strengthened independent authorities to constrain executives \parencite{Keeper2003}. Likewise, citizens alienated from partisan politics (``structural losers'') sometimes prefer technocratic governance over dysfunctional party competition \parencite{Bertsou2020}. These scenarios presume a separation between expertise and ideology. When that presumption fails---when expertise is perceived as aligned---technocratic delegation is no longer a neutral fix but a way to lock in the ruling coalitionŌĆÖs program. In such cases, losers frame technocratic delegation as illegitimate and call to return decisions to ``the people'' via elections or referendums.

%  5
This logic yields clear implications. LosersŌĆÖ resistance to technocratic delegation should intensify in highly polarized, ideologically sorted systems and during periods of sharp governmentŌĆōopposition conflict. It also helps explain why populist movementsŌĆöoften composed of recent electoral or globalization losersŌĆötarget technocratic institutions as part of a corrupt establishment \parencite{Caramani2017}. Conversely, in consensusŌĆÉoriented or depolarized settings, or where technocratic bodies retain crossŌĆÉpartisan legitimacy, backlash should be weaker. The broader paradox follows: institutions designed as impartial checks sustain stability only if they are \emph{seen} as impartial. Once viewed as instruments of those in power, the very actors who must accept themŌĆöthe electoral losersŌĆömay withdraw consent, straining the balance between expertise and democracy.


\section{A Voter's TradeŌĆÉOff: Performance vs.\ Ideological Distance}

Consider a one-dimensional policy space $[0,1]$. A voter $i$ has ideal point $\theta_i \in [0,1]$. The governing coalition (``government'') has position $\theta_g \in [0,1]$. A technocratic institution (``experts'') has perceived position $\theta_e \in [0,1]$. Let $F(\cdot)$ denote the cumulative distribution of voter ideal points. 

\paragraph{Distance and alignment.}
Define voterŌĆōgovernment distance

\[
d_i \;\equiv\; |\theta_i - \theta_g| \in [0,1],
\]

\noindent which corresponds to our empirical measure \texttt{Government distance}. Perceived expert \emph{alignment} with government is summarized by $\alpha \in [0,1]$ via

\[
\theta_e(\alpha) \;=\; \alpha\,\theta_g + (1-\alpha)\,\bar\theta,
\]

\noindent where $\bar\theta$ is a nonŌĆōpartisan reference (e.g., a technocratic consensus or the median policy). Full alignment means $\alpha=1$ (so $\theta_e=\theta_g$); lower $\alpha$ implies more neutral experts.

\paragraph{Sequence.}
Voter $i$ chooses between retaining the status quo of electedŌĆōpolitician control ($S$) or \emph{delegating} to experts ($D$). Under $D$, policy is set at $\theta_e(\alpha)$

\paragraph{Preferences.} Utility is spatial with a perceived technocratic performance/competence gain $B\ge 0$:



\begin{align*}
U_i(S) &= -(\theta_i - \theta_S)^2,\\
U_i(D) &= -(\theta_i - \theta_e(\alpha))^2.
\end{align*}

Delegation only affects outcomes through the location of the implemented policy.

\paragraph{Indifferent voter.}

The indifferent voter satisfies $U_i(D)=U_i(S)$, which implies:

\[
(\theta_i - \theta_e(\alpha))^2 = (\theta_i - \theta_S)^2.
\]

Hence, the cutoff voter is simply the midpoint between the delegated policy and the status quo:

\[
\theta_i^\star(\alpha)
=
\frac{\theta_e(\alpha)+\theta_S}{2}.
\]

Substituting for $\theta_e(\alpha)$:

\[
\theta_i^\star(\alpha)
=
\frac{\alpha\theta_g+(1-\alpha)\bar{\theta}+\theta_S}{2}.
\]


Delegation is adopted under simple majority rule if and only if:

\[
\begin{cases}
 \dfrac{1}{2} > F\!\left(\theta_i^\star(\alpha)\right) 
& \text{if } \theta_e(\alpha) > \theta_S, \\[8pt]
\dfrac{1}{2} < F\!\left(\theta_i^\star(\alpha)\right) 
& \text{if } \theta_e(\alpha) < \theta_S.
\end{cases}
\]

\paragraph{When does an increase in $\alpha$ raise support for delegation?}

The answer depends on the relative position of the delegated policy with respect to the status quo.

If $\theta_e(\alpha) > \theta_S$, voters with ideal points $\theta_i > \theta_i^\star(\alpha)$ support delegation. In this case, an increase in alignment raises support if and only if the indifferent voter shifts to the left, that is,
\[
\frac{\partial \theta_i^\star(\alpha)}{\partial \alpha} < 0.
\]
Since
\[
\frac{\partial \theta_i^\star(\alpha)}{\partial \alpha}
=
\frac{1}{2}\big(\theta_g - \bar{\theta}\big),
\]
this occurs whenever
\[
\theta_g < \bar{\theta}.
\]
Intuitively, if the government is located to the left of the technocratic reference point, greater alignment shifts the delegated policy leftward. When the delegated policy lies to the right of the status quo, this movement brings it closer to the electoral center and expands the set of voters who prefer delegation.

Conversely, if $\theta_e(\alpha) < \theta_S$, voters with $\theta_i < \theta_i^\star(\alpha)$ support delegation. In this case, an increase in alignment raises support if and only if
\[
\frac{\partial \theta_i^\star(\alpha)}{\partial \alpha} > 0,
\]
which holds whenever
\[
\theta_g > \bar{\theta}.
\]
If the government is located to the right of the technocratic benchmark, greater alignment shifts the delegated policy rightward. When this policy initially lies to the left of the status quo, the movement brings it closer to the electoral center and increases the coalition in favor of delegation.

Taken together, these cases imply that alignment increases support for delegation precisely when it moves the delegated policy toward the side of the electorate that already prefers delegation relative to the status quo. When alignment instead pushes policy away from that side, support contracts.

The model yields two broader implications. First, when expert institutions are perceived as highly aligned with the government, support for delegation declines monotonically with ideological distance from the government: the farther a voter is from the governmentŌĆÖs position, the less willing she is to delegate. Second, this distance penalty attenuates when experts are perceived as more neutral or closer to voters than to the government, because delegation is then evaluated less as an extension of the governing coalition and more in terms of relative policy proximity to the status quo.


\section{Data and Findings}

The data comes from an original survey conducted in Finland ($n = 1,515$), with statistical quotas applied for age, gender and region of residence.\footnote{Data collection took place between January and February 2025 through an online panel recruited by Qualtrics. This study complied with standard ethical guidelines for survey research, including informed consent and respondent anonymity; data collection followed the ethical infrastructure and data-protection practices of \fbox{\phantom{\AA bo Akademi}} University. Formal ethical review was not required as the study involved no experimental intervention.}

\subsection{Measurement}

The main independent variable is \texttt{Government distance}, a respondentŌĆōgovernment distance measure based on party-closeness ratings.\footnote{Because \texttt{Government distance} is constructed from respondents' party-closeness ratings to governing parties, it already captures partisan orientation and its intensity in a continuous, coalition-specific way.}

Let $i$ index respondents and let $p \in G$ index parties in the governing coalition. Survey respondents rated their closeness to each parliamentary party on a $0$ŌĆō$10$ scale. Let $C_{ip} \in [0,10]$ denote respondent $i$'s closeness to party $p$. Let $s_p$ be party $p$'s seat count in the \emph{Eduskunta} (the Finnish Parliament) at the time of fieldwork,\footnote{KOK $=48$, PS $=46$, RKP $=9$, KD $=5$.} and define coalition seat weights

\begin{equation}
w_p \equiv \frac{s_p}{\sum_{q \in G} s_q}.
\label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}

Due to missingness, weights were renormalized over the set of parties that respondent $i$ rated:

\begin{equation}
\tilde w_{ip} \equiv
\frac{w_p \,\mathbbm{1}\!\bigl\{\text{$C_{ip}$ observed}\bigr\}}
{\sum_{q \in G} w_q \,\mathbbm{1}\!\bigl\{\text{$C_{iq}$ observed}\bigr\}}.
\label{eq:renorm}
\end{equation}

Then, seatŌĆōweighted coalition closeness is

\begin{equation}
{\texttt{Government closeness}}_i \equiv \sum_{p \in G} \tilde w_{ip}\, C_{ip} \;\in\; [0,10].
\label{eq:govclose}
\end{equation}

We scaled it to $[0,1]$ and defined the complementary distance index:

\begin{equation}
{\texttt{Government distance}}_i \equiv 1 - \frac{{\texttt{Government closeness}}_i}{10}
\;\in\; [0,1].
\label{eq:govdist}
\end{equation}

By construction, larger ${\texttt{Government distance}}_i$ indicates greater distance from the governing coalition.

Beyond the seatŌĆōweighted baseline in \autoref{eq:govdist}, we also construct two conceptually adjacent indices. First, an \emph{unweighted} coalition proximity treats each governing party symmetrically, abstracting from parliamentary strength and thereby reducing dependence on the specific seat distribution at fieldwork. Let

\[
\texttt{Government closeness (UW)}_i \;\equiv\;
\frac{\sum_{p\in G} C_{ip}\,\mathbbm{1}\!\bigl\{\text{$C_{ip}$ observed}\bigr\}}
     {\sum_{q\in G} \mathbbm{1}\!\bigl\{\text{$C_{iq}$ observed}\bigr\}}
\;\in\;[0,10],
\label{eq:govclose_u}
\tag{A1}
\]

with \texttt{NA} if no governing party was rated. The associated distance is

\begin{equation}
\texttt{Government distance (UW)}_i \;\equiv\;
1 - \frac{\texttt{Government closeness (UW)}_i}{10}
\;\in\;[0,1].
\label{eq:govdist_u}
\end{equation}

Second, a \emph{closestŌĆōparty} distance captures the heuristic that respondents may anchor evaluations on the single governing party they feel closest to (salience of a primary partisan tie), and it is maximally robust to selective answering:

\begin{equation}
\texttt{Government distance (Closest)}_i \;\equiv\;
1 - \frac{\max_{p\in G}\, C_{ip}}{10}
\;\in\;[0,1],
\label{eq:govdist_min}
\end{equation}

set to \texttt{NA} when all $C_{ip}$ for $p\in G$ are missing.

Taken together, the seatŌĆōweighted (\autoref{eq:govdist}), unweighted (\autoref{eq:govdist_u}), and closestŌĆōparty (\autoref{eq:govdist_min}) indices map, respectively, to influenceŌĆōweighted proximity to the governing bloc, an equalŌĆōparty aggregation, and a strongestŌĆōaffinity anchor. We use all three in the empirical analysis; as shown below, they yield substantively similar patterns, indicating that our results do not hinge on a particular distance construction or on the treatment of item nonresponse.

\subsection{Estimation Strategy}

We estimated the association between support for technocratic delegation (\texttt{Technocracy}) and ideological distance from the governing coalition (\texttt{Government distance}) using a linear specification. Now, let $\texttt{Technocracy}_i$ denote respondent $i$'s technocracy item and ${\texttt{Government distance}}_i$ the normalized index in \autoref{eq:govdist}.\footnote{We treated the $1$ŌĆō$5$ outcome variable as approximately continuous (ordered models are reported as robustness in \autoref{tab:ologit-models}, yielding substantively identical conclusions). The survey question asked (originally in Finnish) ``What do you think about the following statements? [...] Our country would work better if political decisions were left to independent, non-elected experts instead of politicians.'' The options were ``Strongly disagree'' (n=133), ``Somewhat disagree'' (n=248), ``Neither agree nor disagree'' (n=383), ``Somewhat agree'' (n=436) and ``Totally agree'' (n=197).} 

For expositional clarity we use the seatŌĆōweighted index as our primary measure, since it mirrors partiesŌĆÖ relative bargaining power within the cabinet and thus the theoretical object of interestŌĆöproximity to the governing bloc as policy setter. It also tempers noise from idiosyncratic ratings of very small coalition partners with limited agenda influence. For robustness, we reŌĆōestimate all models with the unweighted aggregation (\autoref{eq:govdist_u}) and the closestŌĆōparty anchor (\autoref{eq:govdist_min}); the results are substantively unchanged (\autoref{tab:ols-models}). The baseline model is



\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\texttt{Technocracy}_i \;=\;& \beta_0 \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_1\,\texttt{Government distance}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_2\,\texttt{Democratic satisfaction}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_3\,\texttt{Trust in politicians}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \boldsymbol{\gamma}'\,\mathbf{X}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \delta_{\text{region}(i)} \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \varepsilon_i \,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:model}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{X}_i$ includes education, age, and gender, and $\delta_{\text{region}(i)}$ are
region fixed effects. The coefficient of interest is $\beta_1$, which---under the formal modelŌĆÖs
assumptions---should be negative when expert institutions are perceived as aligned with the incumbent bloc.


\subsection{Results}

\autoref{fig:pred} plots marginal-standardized (average) predictions of expert support across the full range of distance from the current Finnish Government.\footnote{The solid line is the point estimate and the band the 95\% CI.} Effects are modest but meaningful in a hard case like Finland. Across all three panels---seat-weighted, unweighted, and closest-party---the relationship is monotonically negative: moving from full proximity to maximal distance from the Government lowered the predicted score from roughly 3.5 to about 2.8 on the 1-5 scale. The near-identical slope in the unweighted panel shows that the pattern did not hinge on coalition seat shares or renormalization, while the closest-party panel reproduced the same decline.

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{figure}

{\centering \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure/pred-1} 

}

\caption[Average predicted]{Average predicted: technocracy vs.\ government distance (95\% CIs).}\label{fig:pred}
\end{figure}

\end{knitrout}

In light of our voter-choice framework, these estimates are consistent with a mechanism in which ideological loss rises with distance while the perceived performance gain \(B\) is held fixed. As distance widens, \((\theta_i-\theta_e)^2\) increases, so fewer respondents clear the technocratic delegation threshold \(B>(\theta_i-\theta_e)^2\). Substantively, the slope moves the average respondent from slight technocratic sympathy toward skepticism as distance increases.


Although the survey did not directly ask whether experts were perceived as ideologically biased, the gradient in \autoref{fig:pred} is unlikely to be an artifact of measurement or data aggregation, suggesting that experts were not viewed as uniformly neutral. The pattern implies that a nontrivial share of respondents inferred some alignment between expert bodies and the right-leaning cabinet, which depressed willingness to delegate among those farthest from the government in ideological space. At the same time, these results do not suggest that Finnish voters, on average, saw experts as politicized. We believe that Finland's high baseline of institutional trust coexists with different heterogeneity levels: a considerable number of respondents likely trusted experts, while others---disproportionately opposition-aligned voters---appeared more sensitive to who would be empowered and who appointed them. 

The near-parallel slopes across the seat-weighted, unweighted, and closest-party panels, together with ordered-logit estimates that treat the outcome as ordinal (\autoref{tab:ologit-models}), indicate that the core association is not an artifact of proximity aggregation or outcome scaling. 


\paragraph{Perceived Government-Expert Alignment}

Because we do not directly observe whether respondents think expert bodies are aligned with the government, we proxy perceived alignment with a relative trust differential between the Cabinet and a flagship expert institution (the Bank of Finland).\footnote{The survey question asked (originally in Finnish) ``How much do you trust or distrust the following? Please rate each on a scale of 0 to 10, where 1 is `I don't at all' and 10 is `I trust completely' [Bank of Finland].'' The distribution is: 0 = 34, 1 = 28, 2 = 51, 3 = 67, 4 = 126, 5 = 106, 6 = 220, 7 = 360, 8 = 281, 9 = NA, 10 = NA.} The logic is comparative: if a respondent trusts the Cabinet more than the Bank of Finland, she is more likely to infer that expert agencies will follow the government's lead; if she trusts the Bank of Finland more, she is more likely to view experts as independent. This rests on a reasonable behavioral assumption: Cabinets appoint senior agency officials, set mandates and control budgets. We assume that relative trust therefore serves as a good-enough proxy for who effectively holds influence and control. Then, higher trust in the Cabinet implies that respondents expect experts at the Bank of Finland to align with the government's program, even when preferences diverge. In turn, higher trust in the Bank of Finland signals perceived independence from the government. For example, after a Cabinet announces a new fiscal policy, respondents who trust the Cabinet more will expect the agency to carry out that program; respondents who trust the Bank of Finland more will expect the appointee to follow established standards, even if ministers prefer otherwise. In short, if someone trusts the Cabinet more than the Central Bank, they treat the bank as part of government and expect it to follow; if they trust the Bank of Finland more than the Cabinet, they see it as standing apart and acting independently.


Formally, let $T_i^G$ denote trust in the current government ($0$ŌĆō$10$) and $T_i^E$ trust in the Bank of Finland. Define the signed gap

\[
\texttt{Align}_i \equiv T_i^G - T_i^E.
\]

For exposition we map this to the unit interval and, for estimation, center it at the indifference point:

\[
\texttt{Align}^{01}_i \in [0,1], \qquad
\texttt{Align}^{01}_{i,c} \equiv \texttt{Align}^{01}_i - 0.5 \in [-0.5,0.5].
\]

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{figure}[H]

{\centering \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure/pred.4-1} 

}

\caption[Interactive OLS hypothesis]{Interactive OLS hypothesis: Technocracy $\times$ Government distance moderated by Gov-Expert trust gap}\label{fig:pred.4}
\end{figure}

\end{knitrout}

By construction, $\texttt{Align}^{01}_{i,c}=0$ indicates indifference ($T_i^G \approx T_i^E$); positive values indicate a government-over-experts premium ($T_i^G>T_i^E$), and negative values indicate an experts-over-government premium ($T_i^E>T_i^G$). Using the gap rather than the levels $T_i^G$ and $T_i^E$ reduces sensitivity to response-style shifts (e.g., $(8,7)$ and $(2,1)$ imply the same comparative orientation) and focuses the interaction on perceived alignment rather than absolute institutional trust.

We estimate,

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\texttt{Technocracy}_i \;=\;& \beta_0 \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_1\,\texttt{Government distance}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_2\,\texttt{Align}^{01}_{i,c} \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \beta_3\,\bigl(\texttt{Government distance}_i \times \texttt{Align}^{01}_{i,c}\bigr) \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \boldsymbol{\gamma}'\,\mathbf{X}_i \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \delta_{\text{region}(i)} \\[2pt]
&\;+\; \varepsilon_i \, ,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:model-interaction}
\end{equation}

and plot marginal predictions at $\texttt{Align}^{01}_{i,c} \in \{-0.25,\,0,\,+0.25\}$, labeled in the legend as ``Experts trusted'' (negative gap), ``Indifferent'' ($0$), and ``Government trusted'' (positive gap).\footnote{We follow \textcite[73]{Brambor2006} and interpret the interaction via marginal effects rather than coefficient signs in the regression table (\autoref{tab:interact-models}).} 

In \autoref{fig:pred.4}, the line for respondents who trust the Government more than the Bank of Finland slopes downward: the farther they are from the Government, the less they support delegating to experts. The line for respondents who are indifferent is only mildly downward, showing a much weaker drop. The line for respondents who trust the Bank of Finland more is slightly upward: moving farther from the Government slightly increases support for technocratic delegation. Substantively, distance depresses support for technocratic delegation only when people think experts will follow the GovernmentŌĆÖs lead; when experts are viewed as independent, distance has as a slithglty positive effect on support for technocratic delegation. Substantively, this means that losers' consent to technocracy is conditional: voters distant from the governing bloc withdraw support for technocratic delegation when they expect expert bodies to carry the government's program, but they do not do so when experts are perceived as independent. The interaction therefore isolates the mechanism our theory posits---ideological distance operates through perceived alignment rather than as a uniform aversion to expertise.



\section{Discussion and Limitations}

This study argued that citizens evaluated technocratic delegation through an ideologically valenced lens. We formalized a voter's choice as a trade-off between a perceived performance gain from expert rule, the ideological loss from ceding authority to actors aligned with the incumbent coalition. The model yielded two central implications: support for technocratic delegation decreased with ideological distance from government when experts were perceived as aligned and this distance penalty attenuated when experts were viewed as neutral. Empirically, we analyzed a novel Finnish survey and constructed respondent-level distances to the governing coalition using seat-weighted party-closeness, alongside unweighted and closest-party variants. Linear and ordered-logit models consistently indicated a monotonic, negative association between distance and support for technocratic delegation. Average predicted values declined across the full distance range and the pattern replicated under alternative distance constructions, supporting the model's core comparative statics.

The findings were especially noteworthy because Finland constituted a demanding case. Finland reliably ranked very high on institutional trust, and large shares of the public trusted government, civil service, and the broader expert community. In such a context, technocratic delegation would have been expected to be broadly acceptable across partisan lines. Instead, support appeared conditional on perceived alignment: voters ideologically distant from the right-leaning cabinet expressed lower willingness to delegate. This pattern was consistent with the idea that, even in a high-trust polity, a nontrivial segment of the electorate inferred that experts could lean toward the incumbents, thereby activating a partisan calculus. At the same time, the results did not imply that all Finns viewed experts as politicized. Taken together, we find that technocratic delegation was endorsed when the performance gains outweighed ideological losses.

These results come with several caveats. First, although we now operationalize perceived alignment with a  trust differential (government-over-experts trust gap), this remains a proxy rather than a direct measure of whether respondents \emph{believe} experts are government-aligned. The interaction between \texttt{Government distance} and the gap behaves as the theory predicts---strongly negative when $T^{G}_i>T^{E}_i$, attenuated near $T^{G}_i\approx T^{E}_i$, and weak/flat when $T^{G}_i<T^{E}_i$---which provides construct validity, but the gap could also absorb generalized regime affect or performance evaluations. Second, the design is observational and crossŌĆōsectional, so distance and the trust gap may be jointly shaped by latent ideology or antiŌĆōelitism; causal claims should therefore be cautious. Future work should field direct items on perceived capture/appointment trust and agency ideology, and deploy experiments that randomize appointment rules, explicit ideological cues, and performance signals, ideally embedded in panels around salient appointments or cabinet reshuffles.

Third, our distance measure relied on self-reported party-closeness and seat-share weights as a proxy for influence within the cabinet. Nonresponse patterns and measurement error in closeness ratings could introduce noise, and seat weights might imperfectly map onto perceived agenda power. We partially addressed this by reporting unweighted and closest-party variants, which delivered substantively similar results, but additional weighting schemes should be explored in future research. Fourth, the technocracy outcome was a single ordered item. Although results were robust to ordered-logit specifications, single-item measures are vulnerable to wording effects or scale interpretation.  

Fifth, while Finland served as a strong hard test, external validity beyond high-trust, consensus-oriented democracies remained uncertain. The same mechanism could be stronger where baseline trust was lower and polarization higher, but it could also interact with other macro-level variables. Finally, our analyses focused on attitudes; thus, behavioral implications remained to be established.


Several extensions follow. First, future surveys should directly measure perceived expert alignment and credibility of appointment procedures (e.g., transparent vs.\ partisan selection). Experimental vignettes could randomize expert appointment rules, explicit ideological signals, and evidence of performance, allowing a clean test of the model's thresholds. Second, designs that exploit temporal variation---before/after cabinet reshuffles, high-salience appointments, or prominent policy episodes---would sharpen causal inference. Third, comparative designs could improve inferential leverage by testing whether the negative effect of government distance on support for technocratic delegation intensifies when perceived alignment is more salient and accountability costs loom larger. Fourth, within-country heterogeneity deserves closer attention: multilevel models with party or region random slopes, and explicit tests of the distance effect within parties, would clarify whether the mechanism operated similarly among government supporters and among different strands of the opposition. Finally, linking attitudes to behavior---support for institutional reforms, willingness to cede authority in specific domains, or tolerance for curbing expert bodies---would shed light on the political consequences of ideologically valenced technocratic delegation.
